You must have heard that some languages are procedural. Procedural programming because they consists of global variables and functions. Language such as COBOL (Common Business Oriented Language) is fully a procedural language. Everything in COBOL is broken down into number of functions spread across different files. Everything around us falls to a category, i.e. a type. All we see around us are objects of those types. Objects consist of number of data elements and the operations defined on those data members. Both data and its operations for objects in an Object-Oriented programming stay together as a capsule.

The success of the OO programming is entirely depends upon how good the designer and programmer in OO concepts. Only an OO language such as Java can support an organization towards achieving that goal and only by proper implementation of OO concepts.

What is a Class?

To understand the analogy of a class, we are going to consider a better example - "cars". It is a physical entity or type. For example, all four-wheel vehicles fitted with an Engine belong to a particular category called "Car". All two-wheel vehicles fitted with an engine are usually called "Motor Cycles". One can easily understand, at least one thing is common to them - they all belong to a particular category or type - "car" or "motor cycle". However, type like "car" or "motor cycle" does not describe your particular car or motor cycle. We might also say that the "BMW" is a special type of car. You perhaps own a "BMW" car and that is gray color, year-2003 model, four doors, etc. Someone may have owned a "BMW" car with silver color, year-2002 model, and two doors. You perhaps got some idea about what do I mean by "BMW" and "your particular gray, 2003, 4-door BMW car". What we are saying here is that the "BMW" is a type of "car". In software world, a type is known as a class. Therefore, "BMW" can be considered as a class of "car".

Similarly, we often say, "penguins is a type of bird". When "bird" is the general type, penguins is the special type of bird. Knowingly or unknowingly, we somehow have the knowledge of a type.

Figure – 2.1: A class is a type, similar to a factory capable of producing new.

A class is like a factory that is capable of producing instances. Attributes value of each of such instances varies. For example, a car factory produces new cars. More specifically, a BMW factory can produce new BMW cars of different colors and models.

Let us take one more example. Similar to the "car" class, "BankAccount" is again considered as a class. A "BankAccount" factory can produce new bank account identified by an account number, name, address, and more attributes.

 

What is an Object?

In order to understand about objects let us look at the illustration next. It explains that the factory at the middle is the "Car" class from where all the new BMWs are produced.

Figure – 2.2: A "BMW" class is a factory produces new BMWs.

The little squares inside the object spaces are indicative of different values of the attributes for the variety model of BMW car created by the factory. Examples of such attributes are the color of the car, number of doors, engine capacity, and so on.

Following is the general syntax for creating objects ("your individual car") from the "Car" factory, i.e. class.

Car myCar = new Car();

In Java, a class name starts with a capital letter. However, there is no bar in naming the class with all small letters. In diagram shows six different cars are created from the BMW car factory.

Value of a particular attribute of "your particular car" instance might change, if you repaint it with a different color. Therefore, the class that created it must have methods so that the color once changed can be changed in the system too. Usually, there is always a setter and getter method for each particular attribute of the object. For example, to change the color information of the car in the system the Car class might have getColor and setColor methods. Therefore, myCar needs to be initialized with appropriate attribute values shown next.

Listing – 2.1: Object creation syntax

Car myCar = new car();
myCar.setMake("BMW");
myCar.setModel("M3");
myCar.setColor("Gray");
