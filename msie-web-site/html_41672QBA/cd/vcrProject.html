Foreword

For this project, I originally chose an Inventory System, loosely based on an apparel distribution business.

However, it quickly became clear to me that I have very little to say about inventory systems. I am much more interested in the ideas, concepts and technologies that facilitate an interface between different applications, allowing the real-time usage of data between incompatible systems.

Most businesses of today have multiple software packages performing largely separate tasks on systems that are largely incompatible. These systems may be internal or external to a business as the effects are the same, namely considerable limits on the ability of the software to perform business essential tasks. The solutions to these limitations involve people providing that interface, usually requiring a considerable human involvement, usually introducing a lot of human error.

This is an example of people providing the technology. Software should be the technology; it should provide the smarts, leaving people to perform human related tasks.

Some businesses have attempted to solve the problems by buying an integrated suite of products. These tend to very expensive to purchase, implement and maintain.

To a business, the software application is a subsystem to the business. They don't know and don't care about incompatible systems. If they are incompatible, they shouldn't be!

I am most keen to examine the implications of the advent of web services. It is not the technology per se that interests me. It is what can be done with it, and what are the consequences.

The true promise of web services is that independent, unrelated software packages can be integrated without having to make big changes to any package. Thus applications behave as modules or subsystems within the context of a business's business software solution. Hands off software interactions could change the way we think, and most surely the software we buy.

An opportunity for me is to produce a package that performs various tasks, and interface with other products to perform other tasks. This looks to me to be a brave new world, full of opportunities for those who are willing to make the journey.

I thus chose to consider a distribution business and to design software to address its needs. I created a fictional business called Advanced Products Manufacturing or APM, and characters who perform key roles within APM. I identified their goals and attempted to understand their interactions with the software through the creation of stories.

This led me to design a product around the concept of roles. A role is identified by a group of tasks, for example Sales. Human users (or just users) would be assigned their own login object to gain access to the system, their user name providing access to subsystems through the belonging to a role or roles.

An interesting consequence of this design is that there is no need for the application to have a single point of entry. In fact, there is no single application, or rather, there doesn't need to be. This is not the case in traditional application design.

Control of the user through the use of his roles leads to the next interesting thought. Each user's interface needs to be dynamic, adjusting at least somewhat to his capabilities, as well as his needs as identified by his goals. This also flies in the face of traditional software design as well as creating a number of challenges.

If the user interface were dynamic, defining and controlling that interface would create serious development problems. Testing a user interface is always very hard and incredibly time consuming. A dynamic user interface would test the professionalism, to say nothing of the resolve, of even the most patient and skilled tester.

It would appear then, that the dynamic interface will require some design gymnastics, and doubtless some compromises, if a design is to be successful.

Despite the difficulties, I chose this approach because I wanted to see where it might lead, to learn what I can, to determine the issues and to maybe even resolve them.

The user interface issues results in the need to design the business intelligence into the back end. I propose to implement much of the business intelligence user interface as an alerts subsystem.

Strategy

Employ a top down approach, starting with the role types. Model tasks as objects, modularized and encapsulated. Define the interfaces, noting where the interface is external. The end result should be pieces that fit together and look like one.

Assumptions

  • I have assumed throughout that the application would be browser based. This is because I wanted to discover, through the design of a product, the issues relating to the development of such an application in the browser environment. The designs will also work in a traditional GUI environment, doubtless made prettier and a lot more functional, but would work much the same anyway. I have further assumed the application would not be an applet, although it could be, for the same reasons as above.
  • The designs assume that many businesses use old equipment, and so the browser version may be old.
  • As an aid to the design process, I have assumed that many items would be implemented as objects. For example, Login, Name, Address, Alerts, Tracking. They will need to be defined at some point in the design process, but they would be implemented as objects in any case.
  • I have assumed the product needs to be localizable. The choice of an Address object is a consequence of that, as is not keying off strings.
  • It is expected that many of the users of the system will be relative computer novices. The user interface will need to be simple and easy to use.
  • I have assumed no particular market for the product and so the designs need to be easily adapted to other businesses. I thus need a generalized solution.
  • All items entering or leaving the warehouse are marked with a barcode consistent with have a predetermined barcode system.
  • All suppliers, manufacturers and retailers use the same barcode system.
  • The barcode will live with the item until a paying customer removes it.
  • I have assumed a web services interface to other systems. These include accounting and shipment tracking systems which are probably internal systems, and also to suppliers, manufacturers and retailers that are not. I have defined each interface as a Web Services Object, thus delaying the precise definition of these interfaces until I have a better understanding of the capabilities of the technology.
  • The designs and documents are not complete, nor are they intended to be. If the project were to be taken to the next step, the designs would need to be completed, all issues and inconsistencies resolved, the documentation completed.
  • I am clear that I do not currently possess the skills necessary to understand exactly what can be implemented as a user interface in a browser. From a quick peek at next terms books, I think this knowledge will be coming soon.
  • My knowledge of databases is not sufficient to determine the design, and maybe the feasibility of some database objects

Requirements

  • Manage the inventory of a business.
  • Record all transactions
  • Record all item movements.
  • Conduct thorough error checking.
  • Ensure the integrity of the data.
  • Suppliers provide inventory items.
  • Items may be distributed to manufacturers, distributors or retailers.
  • Items may be distributed as a consignment, which may be returned if not sold.
  • Items may be distributed as a sale, to be invoiced later.
  • Items may be sold online through the distributors web site.
  • Keep track of all inventories.
  • Keep track of bids.
  • Keep track of orders.
  • Keep track of shipments, both entering and leaving.
  • Use the company logo and make the product accessible from the company web site.
  • The user interface must be clear, simple and easy to use. User training curves must be reduced.
  • Each user role interface needs to make it clear who is using the system, and how they use it.
  • Integrate with an accounting package.
  • Provide web services access to its inventory and customer records, as other products will require such access.
  • Be consistent with other web services based products.
  • Differentiate user types by roles, the roles being limited to their own tasks, accessibility to data controlled by access, and by view.
  • There will be a person assigned as the super user role who, it is assumed, will be knowledgeable with regard to the software.
  • Recognize different roles and show an appropriate interface for that user.
  • Address the needs of each user type, facilitating great user efficiency, and to inform the user of pending issues and problems, all in a user configurable manner.
  • All items need to be categorized, that is, all it's properties need to be definable within a constructed environment. For example, for an item that needs to be assigned a color, the list of available colors must be predefined and controlled.
  • Properties are to be implemented as Characteristics. They are user configurable, and theoretically infinite.
  • The user interface must be kept simple to program.
  • The intelligent part of the system needs to be built into the back end (server).
  • A simple interface must connect the user interface with the business logic in the server.
  • System configuration issues will be reserved for a user type who it is assumed will be knowledgeable with regard to the product.
  • The server subsystem will watch the system. It will issue alerts to the appropriate role(s) for system defined conditions and also for user defined alert conditions.
  • Alerts conditions will include inventory item low, shipment late, payment late conditions.
  • The alerts system will need to be continually designed during the development phase of the project.
  • The supplier via Web Services will upload shipment details before the shipment arrives.
  • Only when a transaction has been completed can the related information be archived.
  • The archive information and format must be chosen carefully to allow easy access by tax accounting software, marketing and legal software. These issues are beyond the scope of this project.

A system of this type is a huge undertaking. It is clearly not possible for me to complete the whole product and so, I must carefully create divisions that at least allow me to make some progress towards completing the goals I have set for the project.

In the interests of time, and my sanity, I have chosen to break down the project into pieces defined by the required roles, plus the role of a super user.

  • Warehouse Manager - Shipping
  • Inventory Manager - Inventory
  • Product Manager – Bids, Offers, Contracts, Products, Catalog, Suppliers
  • Sales Manager.
  • Marketing Manager.
  • Super User Role – Permissions System, System Configuration, Alerts System.

Competitive Review

I have spent many long hours looking for web based inventory systems. There are very few, and they are not available as demo systems. I am thus limited to non-web based systems, many of which are very simplistic. The more sophisticated systems are not available as downloads.

I could conduct a review of these products but I feel the gain, if any, would not be worth the effort. However, there are some interesting points:

  • They are all monolithic applications.
  • They have a single interface, which all users need to navigate.
  • The interfaces are very cluttered, partly because there is not a recognition that the information being presented is not tailored to the viewer.
  • They are not easy to use.
  • Once learnt, the systems are probably fairly easy to use, the learning curve being a problem.
  • They mostly do not handle multiple concurrency issues.
  • They are not hi-tech designs, solutions or implementations.
  • They are not web based.
  • They do not implement Web Services. I did see an article about a company that implemented a Web Services interface between itself and a supplier. They raved about it. Saved them lots of money, they claimed.
  • They usually do not implement a RDBMS.
  • To compete, they usually integrate with software from other suppliers. Due to the difficulties of integration, this appears to be as much a liability as an asset.

Luigi

 

"I must be able to plan the future of my business. I need to track my shipments and my money."

 

Luigi has been in the business all his life. His business, Asconi Products, was founded by his great grandfather Carlo, a poor Sicilian immigrant. Luigi was groomed from childhood to run the family business. He has continued to grow the business, despite some considerable business and family problems, and is widely respected in the business. His customers are mostly medium sized distributors and remanufacturors, all of them based in North America. His insistence on only using the finest materials, employing highly skilled craftsmen and his endless demands for quality have led his company to be widely regarded as a producer of high quality products.

 

Luigi is married to Marie, and they have four children, to whom some day, he hopes to pass the business.

Luigi begins his day the way he always does. He gets up, takes a five mile run, goes home, showers, has breakfast, kisses his wife and children goodbye, and arrives at his office before 7am. His many issues today include problems with the ongoing expansion of his manufacturing facilities, problems securing the quality raw materials from his main supplier and preparing for an end of month meeting with his bankers. However, his top priority issue is to ensure the latest large shipment to his primary customer passes the inspection checks today, cash flow always being a primary concern.

From his ever-present laptop, he starts his browser and selects the shortcut to the APM Web Site, from where he selects the Supplier Portal. He securely enters his username and password. He sees an alert from APM which he checks. It states that the shipment passed inventory checks late yesterday and that the invoice will be paid later today. Somewhat relieved, he checks the status of the next order which he expects to receive in the next few weeks. There is no further progress, just as he expected.

Just then the phone rings, his Sales Manager has some good news.......

Howard - Warehouse Manager

 

"I need to quickly pass into inventory all that belongs there, and return the rest"

 

Howard has been in manufacturing all his life. He started on the factory floor and has progressed through foreman to management. His knowledge of all aspects of the manufacturing and distributions systems have made him a valuable asset to the company. Howard's quick eagle eye for quality allows him to spot defects from before it gets anywhere near the warehouse, or so claim his many admirers, and his determination to pass only top quality products is vital to the success of APM.

 

Howard is divorced and has 2 grown children. His hobbies include hunting and fishing.

Howard has been unusually busy the last few weeks. With stocking up for the new season and tracking the remanufactured items, his plate has been full.

 

Howard's staff are checking a supply of items that arrived only yesterday, a shipment that has been of consider interest to the head of sales. Howard is feeling the heat and frequently checks their progress, to the point of meddling, some say.

 

Howard's logs on to the system and lists the items that have passed inspection. "Nearly done" he thinks to himself, "I can't wait to get Richard off my back". Howard then checks the progress of some four shipments he is expecting over the next week. He notes the expected shipping dates and begins to plan accordingly.

Doug - Inventory Manager

 

"I need to keep track of everything that comes in and everything that goes out. We need to keep a stock, not too much, and not too little. "

"I need to know where the fish are biting and what they are biting on"

 

Richard is a born salesman. He eats and drinks the sales game. He loves the chase and nothing beats the thrill of closing the sale. Permissions

Permissions within the application are defined by roles. A role is granted read, write and/or delete access to a subsystem. Further, users are granted a role, or multiple roles, allowing access to that roles access permitted subsystem, with the grants of that role.

There are roles that are defined by the system, for example SuperUser Role, and roles that are user defined. For example, a role FactorySales could be created with read access to remanufacturing information. A user granted the FactorySales role would thus be permitted access to remanufacturing information.

This design allows for almost complete control over all users of the application.

A user called the super user owns the permissions subsystem. The super user:

  • has all rights to all parts of all subsystems.
  • Assigns permissions to subsystems by roles and/or users.
  • Assigns users to roles.
  • Assigns permissions to roles and/or to users within those roles.

Each user, in effect, has a permissions object. The software will retrieve this object. Menu choices will be determined by the choices available to the user. The user interface will be dynamic and based upon the tasks the user is permitted to perform.

Available access types are:

  • Read (select)
  • Write (read and write)
  • Delete

Lessons Learned

  • be careful what you expect from others

  • there exists not a molehill that I cannot turn into a mountain.

  • there exists not a mountain that can, from afar, look like a molehill.

  • people are incredibly clever....

  • brainstorming sessions make the creative process so much easier, and a lot more fun...

  • brainstorming sessions can go almost anywhere as the participants tend to have their own biases, experiences and thoughts.

  • design a brainstorming session, decide an issue, decide who to invite, limit the time for each topic.
  • I realized, a little late, that I really have very little to say about inventory systems.
  • I got myself into a tiz over the inventory system. realized that that was not what i had to say - it is the interwoven relationships of dependent independent businesses, how they interact and how their software will help them do that.
  • Extranet - a company's intranet that allows customers access to certain applications within that intranet.
  • Must do prep work - always like to avoid this stuff.
  • Break down the project into smaller logical pieces. Continue until the pieces are manageable.
  • Define each piece, describe how the pieces interact.
  • Providing a web based interface for non-computer literate people is asking for trouble. Such people would probably be better off with an environment which is much more closely controlled.
  • Researching a market is incredibly time consuming.
  • Researching competing products is even more time consuming.
  • This work is hard to measure, is what I am doing working, will it lead anywhere, will I get feedback, from whom, when, how, if ever.....
  • Pushing around tons of paper is not fun.
  • I can draw images illustrating ideas...this is quite easy.
  • Turning images into something more concrete, like a specification is very hard.
  • I hate writing specs!
  • I don't have the skills with graphical tools like Visio to be able to produce decent electronic specs in a sensible amount of time.
